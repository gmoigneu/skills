# Method selector

Use only methods that address the live bottleneck.

| Bottleneck | Method | Core move | Useful prompt |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Unclear reasoning | Paul-Elder elements and standards | Inspect purpose, question, information, assumptions, concepts, inferences, implications, and viewpoints. Test clarity, accuracy, relevance, logic, depth, breadth, significance, precision, and fairness | "Which assumption carries most of this conclusion?" |
| Cognitive bias | Kahneman bias audit | Slow down substitution, framing, anchoring, availability, overconfidence, loss aversion, and base-rate neglect | "What easier question might you be answering instead?" |
| Narrow framing | WRAP | Widen options, reality-test assumptions, attain distance, prepare to be wrong | "What option disappears if this is not an either/or choice?" |
| Uncertain future | Superforecasting | Define a resolvable claim, decompose it, choose a reference class, estimate a base rate, adjust with case evidence, and update | "Among comparable cases, how often does this happen?" |
| Outcome bias | Thinking in bets | Express belief probabilistically and separate decision process from luck | "Would this still be a good decision if the bad outcome occurred?" |
| Hidden failure | Premortem and inversion | Assume failure, reconstruct causes, then prevent or cap them | "It is a year later and this failed. What most likely caused it?" |
| Cascading effects | Second-order thinking | Trace effects of effects across time and actors | "And then what happens, including how others respond?" |
| Hard-to-reverse choice | Reversibility and option value | Compare cost of waiting, acting, reversing, and losing future choices | "Which path preserves the most valuable future options?" |
| Conflicting priorities | Values-first trade-off | Identify non-negotiables and acceptable downsides before scoring | "Which downside are you more willing to own?" |
| Too many qualitative factors | Weighted decision matrix | Limit to material criteria, weight them, score options, and test whether modest weight changes flip the answer | "Which weight would have to change to reverse the ranking?" |
| Missing evidence | Value of information | Ask whether a cheap fact can change the choice. If yes, acquire it before deciding | "What is the cheapest information that could reverse this decision?" |
| Sunk cost or escalation | Kill criteria and tripwires | Set observable exit, pause, or review conditions in advance | "What evidence would make continuing irresponsible?" |
| Stalled execution | Implementation intention | Specify action, owner, time, place, trigger, and first visible output | "What will you do, exactly, by when?" |

## Decision triage

Use three dimensions.

1. **Stakes** from low to high.
2. **Reversibility** from cheap to effectively irreversible.
3. **Urgency** based on whether the cost of delay grows slowly, steadily, or sharply.

- Low stakes with cheap reversal. Decide quickly and set a review trigger.
- High stakes with cheap reversal. Pilot and learn.
- Low stakes with costly reversal. Check lock-in and hidden downside.
- High stakes with costly reversal. Run the full audit, seek outside evidence, and document the choice.

## Evidence ladder

Prefer this order when applicable.

1. Direct observation or reliable measurement.
2. Relevant base rates and comparable cases.
3. Controlled or well-designed empirical evidence.
4. Multiple independent expert judgments with track records.
5. A single expert opinion.
6. Anecdote, intuition, or vivid story.

Do not apply this ordering mechanically to values, relationships, or private experience. First-person preferences may be decisive evidence about what the user values, but not about external causal claims.

## Question discipline

- Ask one question at a time until the frame is stable.
- Summarize every three to five substantive answers or whenever the frame changes.
- Prefer "what evidence" over repeated "why," which can invite rationalization.
- Ask for examples after abstract claims.
- Offer a tentative synthesis when the user is circling, then ask them to attack it.
- Increase pressure on contradictions, unsupported certainty, and avoidance.
- Reduce pressure when facts are sufficient and the remaining choice is legitimately about values.
