# Books and intellectual basis

This skill is a synthesis, not a reproduction of any one author's system. Some books below won major prizes. Others are included because they turn the research into usable decision practices.

## Foundation

| Book | Why it matters | Methods used here | Recognition |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *Thinking, Fast and Slow* by Daniel Kahneman | Best broad account of predictable judgment errors and overconfidence | Bias audit, outside view, base rates, loss aversion, outcome-versus-process caution | Winner of the 2012 National Academies Communication Award and the 2011 Los Angeles Times Book Prize. Kahneman received the Nobel memorial prize in economics |
| *Expert Political Judgment* by Philip E. Tetlock | Shows why unaudited expert prediction performs poorly and why forecasts need accountability | Resolvable forecasts, calibration, tracking, updating | Won the 2008 Grawemeyer Award for Ideas Improving World Order and the Woodrow Wilson Foundation Award |
| *Superforecasting* by Philip E. Tetlock and Dan Gardner | Converts forecasting research into trainable habits | Decomposition, reference classes, probabilistic estimates, frequent updates | Built on Tetlock's prize-winning forecasting research and the Good Judgment Project's large forecasting tournament |
| *Decisive* by Chip Heath and Dan Heath | Most usable general decision-process framework | WRAP, option widening, reality testing, distance, tripwires, "ooching" through small tests | Major bestseller. Selected for practical utility rather than a book prize |
| *Thinking in Bets* by Annie Duke | Makes uncertainty and luck usable in ordinary decisions | Probabilistic beliefs, decision records, resulting and outcome-bias checks | National bestseller. Selected for practical utility rather than a book prize |
| *Quit* by Annie Duke | Corrects escalation and sunk-cost errors | Kill criteria, expected value of continuing, identity and status checks | Selected for practical utility rather than a book prize |
| *How Not to Be Wrong* by Jordan Ellenberg | Builds statistical and mathematical skepticism without requiring advanced math | Nonlinearity, conditional probability, regression to the mean, selection effects | Ellenberg received the Schmidt Award for Excellence in Science Communications |
| *The Miniature Guide to Critical Thinking Concepts and Tools* by Richard Paul and Linda Elder | Supplies a domain-independent structure for examining arguments | Elements of reasoning and universal intellectual standards | Widely cited educational framework. Selected for rigor and applicability rather than a trade-book prize |

## Useful supplements

- *The Scout Mindset* by Julia Galef covers identity-protective reasoning, curiosity, and changing one's mind.
- *Noise* by Daniel Kahneman, Olivier Sibony, and Cass Sunstein covers inconsistency across judgments and decision hygiene.
- *The Great Mental Models, Volume 1* by Shane Parrish and collaborators covers inversion, first principles, second-order thinking, and map/territory distinctions.
- *The Book of Why* by Judea Pearl and Dana Mackenzie covers causal reasoning and why correlation or prediction alone cannot answer intervention questions.
- *Calling Bullshit* by Carl Bergstrom and Jevin West covers misleading quantitative claims, selection effects, and bad visual evidence.
- *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* by Richard Rumelt covers diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent action for strategic work decisions.

## Reading order

For direct practical value

1. *Decisive*
2. *Superforecasting*
3. *Thinking in Bets*
4. *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
5. *Quit*

For a deeper critical-thinking curriculum, add Paul-Elder, *How Not to Be Wrong*, *The Book of Why*, and *Calling Bullshit*.
